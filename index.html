Experience

Study on the Mechanism of B-Te Threshold Switching Materials

Connect:
This project mainly elaborates on the threshold Switching mechanism of B-Te threshold switching materials through theoretical calculations.
My works:

  • I adopted a melt-quenching technique to generate the initial B-Te amorphous structures by AIMD.
  • I conducted electrical excitation simulation on the initial model.The electronic excitation effect was simulated by transferring a given percentage of electrons from the maximum valence band states to the minimum conduction band states. Atomic configuration analyses, including PCF, BAD, and CND, were then performed.
  • I also make some computes about DOS, IPR, MLWF. The DOS was computed using HSE06 hybrid functionals in order to improve the accuracy of band gap calculation. The IPR was computed for each electronic state by measuring and normalizing atomic orbital projections. Smaller IPR values suggest delocalized bulk-like states, whereas greater IPR values indicate stronger localization. To identify bonding configuration change throughout the excitation process, maximally localized Wannier functions (MLWFs) were built. The distinction between LP electron centers and atomic bonding was determined by whether an MLWF center is coupled with a connection between two atoms that is strong enough to be characterized as a bond.

Result:
Via ab initio simulating the electronic excitation process of the amorphous boron tellurides, we observed reversible semiconductor-metal transitions that can reflect the switching principles of the selectors. The transient switching in conductance originates from fine structural tunings, namely, the changes of constraint surrounding the big boron clusters, and conversions between covalent and hypervalent bonding schemes throughout the entire tellurium network. Our work provides much-needed atomistic insight into the threshold switching mechanisms that may enlighten the material design to enable superior selector devices.

Research on the Relevant Principles of ScxSb2Te3 Phase Change Materials

Connect:
This project discovered the ultra-low resistance drift phenomenon of ScxSb2Te3 phase change material and explained the formation principle of its ultra-low resistance drift property through theoretical calculation methods.
My works:

  • I adopted a melt-quenching technique to generate the initial ScxSb2Te3 amorphous structures by AIMD.
  • I computed the angular-limited three-body correlation (ALTBC) from the AIMD trajectories, the distorted bond angle of three-atom-chains, e.g., Te−Sc−Te and Te−Sb−Te, was required in a range from 155° to 180°. The two bond lengths formed by the central atom are r1 and r2.

Result:
We have demonstrated unprecedentedly low resistance drift in emerging cache-type ScxSb2Te3 PCMs and untangled the underlying mechanism via AIMD simulations and 3D APT characterizations. We found that the electronic band structure is hardly altered during aging. Intrinsic chemical heterogeneity of Sc and Sb species at the nanoscale is believed to exert certain constraints from medium- to long-range of the disordered ScxSb2Te3 network, suffocating the atomic motion to freeze the potential structural relaxation. In particular, the local Sc enrichment effectively suppresses the temporal evolvement of Peierls distortion, which may be a key origin for the extremely low resistance drift. With all the superior features like subnanosecond SET speed, low power consumption, fair data retention, and thickness-independent ultralow resistance drift, ScxSb2Te3 (x = 0.2−0.3) serves as a promising candidate for advanced universal memory and in- memory computing.
